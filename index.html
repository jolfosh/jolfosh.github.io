about

why is this site so ugly?

Hold on a second. Isn't this the personal webpage of a software developer? Shouldn't this page be nice and pretty to showcase my awesome web development skills? Well... Yes and No.

First of all, I think this site is beautiful. Maybe it's nostalgia for websites in the early 2000s. Maybe it's the minimalism and simplicity that appeals to me. Maybe it's the elegent JetBrains Mono font. Maybe it's even a breath of fresh air from all the attention grabbing, data sapping, mind manipulating garbage that currently occupies most of the public facing distopian web. Ignoring my social commentary, I've designed this site with the intention of showing the beauty and performance HTML & CSS was created with, so thank you for taking the time to explore my little site.


my software engineering journey

Beginning with my introduction to video games with our familiar plumber friend, I have had a life-long obsession with learning about how things work (mostly video games). This manifested with me tearing open computers and various devices to try to observe and track the beautiful black magic inside. As I ripped apart more and more things (and occasionally put them back together), I began to understand more and more devices without needing to savagely massacre them.

After building my own computers and tinkering as far as my budget would allow, my curiosity eventually shifted towards the digital frontier. I wanted to make video games and write programs for the machines I had so lovingly obsessed over. So after leading the tech department at my highschool for 3 years, writing a thesis on the corruption and inessential incentive of four-year university, achieving an ACT score of 34, and writing an entrence essay on AGI (artificial general intelligence), I attended Oral Roberts University with the intent on studying Computer Information Technology with a full-ride scholarship.

Skipping over the religious trauma and hypocracy, learning software development was just too slow for my indomnable curiosity, so after being denied the possibility to test out of courses or progress faster, my highschool thesis, which had been foreshadowing my destiny, came rushing back to my reality. You guessed it, I dropped out after the first semester. Thus began my journey learning software development alone.

Now, I say alone with a grain of salt. With the amount of resources available for free on the internet, it wouldn't be hard to argue that I had a whole univerity education at my fingertips. I first learned C++ on HackerRank completing all their challenges to learn as I went. I then started to use my newfound powers to write Python scripts to automate parts of my job as an Office Manager for an amazing physical therapy clinic. This gave me a little experience, but after a year or so, a friend of mine was looking to hire a C++/Python Junior Developer for his startup. I, of course, hopped on board faster than my shoes could follow. It was there that I gained an amazing mentor (ex-Intel) who guided me and gave me the space and projects to learn real development best practices and production code.

While, unfortunately the startup was put on an indefinate hold ("the axe"), it set up the experience I needed to break into the field without a college degree. I have now been a software engineer for ~3 years and have had the opportunity to learn and work in many different languages and frameworks. I'm aware that I am still in the beginning of my career, but I am incredibly optimistic for the future and opportunities that may come.
